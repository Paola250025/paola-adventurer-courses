- SAY: "Real quick, because I think this is wild — archery is ancient. The oldest arrowheads ever found are around sixty-four thousand years old. The oldest complete bow — pulled out of a bog in Denmark — is about eight thousand years old. For almost all of human history the bow was a tool for hunting and war; whole empires were built on better bows and better archers — the Mongols are the famous example. When guns showed up it almost disappeared… until European aristocracy revived it as a hobby in the sixteen-hundreds, and that's where the sport we know today comes from. It was Olympic in 1900, '04, '08 and 1920, vanished for fifty-two years, and came back for good in 1972. And my favorite fact: studies say archery is about three times safer than golf — and Bhutan is the only country on earth where it's the official national sport."

EPISODE 2 — The Recurve Bow (~4 min)

[Slide 7 · Title card] ON SCREEN: CHOOSE YOUR BOW — Ep. 2: The Recurve Bow
[Slide 8 · Hook]
- ON SCREEN: A 3,000-year-old design trick
- SAY: "This is probably the bow you already picture when you think 'archery' — and it's hiding a design trick that's over three thousand years old, one that makes it faster without making it bigger."
- B-ROLL: Recurve held up, curved limb tips in focus.
[Slide 9 · History & fun facts]
- ON SCREEN: From the Mongols to the Olympics · design ~1600 BCE · only Olympic bow since 1972
- SAY: "So the recurve design — where the tips of the limbs curve away from you — shows up at least as far back as 1600 BCE in Egypt and the Near East, and some historians trace it even earlier to the Assyrians. Egyptians, Persians, Greeks, Romans, Koreans, Chinese all used it — and above all the Mongols, whose short, powerful recurves fired from horseback helped build the largest empire in history. And that curve is literally where the name comes from: curving the tips away from you lets the bow store more energy without being longer — more power in a bow you can actually handle. Since 1972 it was, for decades, the only bow allowed at the Olympics — compound only joins in a mixed event starting 2028."
- B-ROLL: Close-up of the curved limb tips; slow draw from the side.
[Slide 10 · Parts, simply]
- ON SCREEN: PARTS — Riser (grip) · Limbs (the engine) · String · Arrow rest
- SAY: "Let me keep the parts really simple. The riser is the center piece you hold — it also has the little window you aim through. The limbs bolt on, top and bottom; they bend and store the energy when you pull the string — think of them as the engine. The string connects the two limb tips and is what actually launches the arrow. And the arrow rest is just where the arrow sits before you shoot. One tip that saves beginners money: a lot of recurves are 'takedown,' which means you can swap just the limbs for stronger ones as you get better — you don't buy a whole new bow."
- B-ROLL: Recurve on a stand, camera points to each part as its label appears on screen.
[Slide 11 · Who it's for]
- ON SCREEN: WHO IT'S FOR — loves the classic/Olympic challenge · happy to build good form · wants a bow that grows
- SAY: "So who's the recurve really for? Someone who likes the idea of a classic, technical challenge, is drawn to that Olympic feel, doesn't mind putting in time to build good form, and wants a bow that can grow with them just by swapping the limbs."
- B-ROLL: You at full draw with the recurve, calm and focused.
[Slide 12 · Close → Ep. 3]
- ON SCREEN: NEXT — Ep. 3: The Genesis Bow (used by 4M+ students)
- SAY: "Now — if the recurve sounds good but you want something easier and cheaper to start with, next video I'll show you the bow more than four million students across the U.S. have used to learn from scratch: the Genesis."
- B-ROLL: Genesis bow teaser shot.

EPISODE 3 — The Genesis Bow (~4 min)

[Slide 13 · Title card] ON SCREEN: CHOOSE YOUR BOW — Ep. 3: The Genesis Bow
[Slide 14 · Hook]
- ON SCREEN: One bow that fits (almost) anyone
- SAY: "This is the most one-size-fits-all bow of the three — it was literally designed so a single model fits almost anyone, with no adjustments needed."
- B-ROLL: Genesis bow held up.
[Slide 15 · History & fun facts]
- ON SCREEN: Built for U.S. schools · NASP, Kentucky 2002 · ~10,000 schools · 4M+ students · 47 states, 5 countries
- SAY: "The Genesis was built for a program called NASP — the National Archery in the Schools Program — created in Kentucky in 2002 with the state Department of Education. Today it's in nearly ten thousand schools, more than four million students have gone through it, across forty-seven U.S. states and five countries. And the problem it solved is such a real one: in a class of thirty kids of totally different sizes and strengths, you can't hand out thirty custom-fitted bows — you need one design that just works for everybody."
- B-ROLL: Genesis beauty shot; a class-style setup if you have one.
[Slide 16 · Parts, simply]
- ON SCREEN: PARTS — Single cam · Idler wheel · NO fixed draw length · Adjustable 10–20 lb
- SAY: "Here's what makes it tick. Unlike the compound, the Genesis uses a single cam — that's the one pulley — which gives you a smoother shot with less recoil and less noise. The idler wheel up top just guides the string and needs almost zero maintenance. But here's the big one for beginners: there's no fixed draw length. It doesn't matter how tall you are or how long your arms are — the same bow adjusts to you. And the draw weight adjusts from ten to twenty pounds — light enough to start, but it feels like a thirty-five-pound recurve thanks to the design, so it still feels real."
- B-ROLL: Close-up of the single cam turning; the idler wheel.
[Slide 17 · Who it's for]
- ON SCREEN: WHO IT'S FOR — trying it out · families & kids · groups · lowest cost, lowest maintenance
- SAY: "So who's it for? Honestly, anyone who wants to try archery without a big commitment. Families with kids. Groups. Or anyone who just wants the most affordable, lowest-maintenance option for shooting every now and then."
- B-ROLL: Family-friendly shot; you handing the bow to someone.
[Slide 18 · Close → Ep. 4]
- ON SCREEN: NEXT — Ep. 4: The Compound Bow (born from a hunter's saw)
- SAY: "Now if you're more into hunting, speed, and precision — and you don't mind spending a bit more — you're going to love the next one. It was born from a hunter who wanted his arrow to reach a deer before the animal could even move. I'll tell you his story next video."
- B-ROLL: Compound bow teaser shot.

EPISODE 4 — The Compound Bow (~4–5 min)

[Slide 19 · Title card] ON SCREEN: CHOOSE YOUR BOW — Ep. 4: The Compound Bow
[Slide 20 · Hook]
- ON SCREEN: A saw, a recurve, and airplane pulleys
- SAY: "This bow exists because a hunter in Missouri, back in the nineteen-sixties, grabbed a saw, cut the tips off a recurve, and bolted on airplane pulleys. I'm serious."
- B-ROLL: Compound bow held up, cams in focus.
[Slide 21 · History & fun facts]
- ON SCREEN: Invented by Holless Wilbur Allen · 1966 · patent 1969 · started the modern compound industry
- SAY: "His name was Holless Wilbur Allen, and he invented it in 1966. In his own words, he wanted a bow that could get an arrow to a deer at ten to twenty-five yards before the animal could react. So he cut the tips off a homemade recurve, mounted little aircraft pulleys, and stumbled onto what's called the block-and-tackle effect — way more speed for way less effort. He got the patent in 1969, teamed up with a bowmaker named Tom Jennings to actually manufacture it, and that's the start of the whole modern compound industry. The bittersweet part: Allen died in a car accident in 1979 and never got to see how far his invention would go."
- B-ROLL: Slow pan of the cams and cables.
[Slide 22 · Parts, simply]
- ON SCREEN: PARTS — Cams · Cables · LET-OFF (hold ~10 lb on a 70-lb bow) · Release aid · Peep sight
- SAY: "Let's break it down. The cams are two wheels, one at each limb tip, that rotate as you draw — that's where the mechanical advantage comes from. The cables work with the cams to move the force around. And here's the big difference from the recurve and the Genesis — it's called let-off. Once you reach full draw, the weight you're actually holding drops dramatically, usually sixty-five to ninety percent less. So on a seventy-pound bow with eighty-five percent let-off, at full draw you're only holding about ten or eleven pounds — which means you can sit there and aim calmly without getting tired. Instead of your fingers, compounds almost always fire with a little hand trigger called a release, for consistency. And a peep sight — this tiny ring in the string — lines up with your sight so you anchor at the exact same spot every single time."
- B-ROLL: Cams rotating at full draw; release aid on the string; peep sight lined up.
[Slide 23 · Who it's for]
- ON SCREEN: WHO IT'S FOR — hunting · competitive precision · loves the gear/tech · OK investing more
- SAY: "So who's it for? Someone who wants to hunt, or compete in precision target archery, or honestly just loves the technical, mechanical side of the gear — and doesn't mind a bigger upfront investment in exchange for more speed and accuracy with less strain on your body."
- B-ROLL: You at full draw with the compound, steady aim.
[Slide 24 · Close → Ep. 5]
- ON SCREEN: NEXT — Ep. 5: Which bow is YOURS?
- SAY: "Okay — you've now seen all three. In the last video I'm going to give you a super simple way to figure out, in about two minutes, which of the three is actually yours — and how to take the next step."
- B-ROLL: Three bows lined up again.

[Slide 28 · The 4-question framework]
- ON SCREEN: 1 GOAL · 2 BUDGET · 3 HOW OFTEN · 4 SPACE
- SAY: "Question one — what's your goal? If it's to socialize, get some exercise, or just try it: Genesis or recurve. If you dream about Olympic-style competition: recurve. If it's hunting, or maximum precision with the least physical effort: compound. Question two — your budget? Genesis is the most affordable; the recurve starts low-to-mid and grows with you as you swap limbs; the compound asks for the biggest investment upfront because of the mechanism. Question three — how often will you actually shoot? Every once in a while: Genesis, it's the simplest to maintain. A hobby you want to grow into: recurve. Something serious and frequent, hunting or competing: compound. And question four — what space do you have? All three are great at an indoor range; the compound needs more tuning, the recurve needs room to work on your form, and the Genesis is the simplest to transport and store."

Genesis: for trying it out or families, lowest cost, casual, simplest to store. Compound: hunting or competitive precision, highest investment, serious and frequent, needs more tuning."
