### 3. Ep1 History & fun facts
- ON SCREEN: Older than you think — 64,000-yr-old arrowheads · 8,000-yr-old bow · 3x safer than golf
- SAY: "Archery is ancient. The oldest arrowheads found are around 64,000 years old, and the oldest complete bow ever found — pulled from a bog in Denmark — is about 8,000 years old. For most of human history the bow was a tool of hunting and war; whole empires were built on better bows and archers — the Mongols being the most famous. When firearms arrived it nearly disappeared, until European aristocracy revived it as a pastime in the 1600s — that's where modern sport archery comes from. It was Olympic in 1900, 1904, 1908 and 1920, vanished for 52 years, and came back for good in 1972. Fun fact: studies find archery about 3 times safer than golf — and Bhutan is the only country where it's the official national sport."

### 8. Ep2 History & fun facts
- ON SCREEN: From the Mongols to the Olympics · Recurve design ~1600 BCE · Only Olympic bow since 1972
- SAY: "The recurve design — limb tips curving away from the archer — has evidence dating to at least 1600 BCE in Egypt and the Near East, and some historians trace it to the Assyrians. Egyptians, Persians, Scythians, Greeks, Romans, Koreans, Chinese — and above all the Mongols, whose short, powerful recurves fired from horseback helped build the largest empire in history. The name comes from exactly that curve: it stores more energy without a longer bow — more power in a bow you can actually handle. And since 1972 it was, for decades, the only bow allowed at the Olympics; compound only joins in a mixed event starting 2028."
- B-ROLL: close-up of the curved limb tips; slow draw from the side
### 9. Ep2 Parts explained simply
- ON SCREEN: PARTS — Riser (grip) · Limbs (the engine) · String · Arrow rest
- SAY: "Let's keep the parts simple. The riser is the center piece you hold — it also has the sight window. The limbs bolt on top and bottom; they bend and store the energy when you draw — think of them as the bow's engine. The string connects the limb tips and is what actually launches the arrow. And the arrow rest is just where the arrow sits before the shot. One beginner tip: many recurves are 'takedown' — you can swap just the limbs for stronger ones as you improve, without buying a whole new bow."

### 15. Ep3 Parts explained simply
- ON SCREEN: PARTS — Single cam · Idler wheel · NO fixed draw length · Adjustable 10–20 lb
- SAY: "The parts, simply. Unlike the compound, the Genesis uses a single cam — that's the pulley — giving a smoother shot with less recoil and noise. The idler wheel guides the string and needs almost no maintenance. Here's the big one for beginners: no fixed draw length. It doesn't matter how tall you are or how long your arms are — the same bow adjusts to your draw. And the draw weight adjusts from 10 to 20 pounds — light to start, but it feels like a 35-pound recurve, so it still feels real."

## EP 4 — The Compound Bow (~4-5 min)
### 18. Ep4 Title card
- ON SCREEN: CHOOSE YOUR BOW — Ep. 4: The Compound Bow
### 19. Ep4 Hook
- ON SCREEN: A saw, a recurve, and airplane pulleys
- SAY: "This bow exists because a hunter in Missouri, in the 1960s, grabbed a saw, cut the tips off a recurve, and bolted on airplane pulleys. Seriously."
- B-ROLL: compound bow held up, cams in focus
### 20. Ep4 History & fun facts
- ON SCREEN: Invented by Holless Wilbur Allen · 1966 · patent 1969 · started the modern compound industry
- SAY: "It was invented by Holless Wilbur Allen in 1966. In his own words, he wanted a bow that could get an arrow to a deer at 10 to 25 yards before the animal could move. He cut the tips off a homemade recurve, mounted small aircraft pulleys, and stumbled onto the block-and-tackle effect — more speed with less effort. He got the patent in 1969 and partnered with bowmaker Tom Jennings to manufacture it — the start of the modern compound industry. Bittersweet note: Allen died in a car accident in 1979, never seeing how far his invention would go."
- B-ROLL: slow pan of the cams and cables
### 21. Ep4 Parts explained simply
- ON SCREEN: PARTS — Cams · Cables · LET-OFF (hold ~10 lb on a 70-lb bow) · Release aid · Peep sight
- SAY: "The parts. The cams are two eccentric wheels, one at each limb tip, that rotate as you draw — they're the mechanical advantage. Cables work with the cams to transfer force. Now the big difference from recurve and Genesis: let-off. Once you reach full draw, the weight you hold drops dramatically — typically 65 to 90 percent less. On a 70-pound bow with 85% let-off you're only holding about 10 or 11 pounds at full draw, so you can aim calmly without tiring. Instead of fingers, compounds almost always fire with a mechanical release for consistency, and a peep sight — a little ring — lines up with your sight so you anchor the same spot every time."
- B-ROLL: cams rotating at full draw; release aid on the string; peep sight lined up
